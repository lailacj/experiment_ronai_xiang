In-Context Next-Word Prediction as a Mechanism for Generating
Alternatives
Anonymous TACL submission
Abstract
1 Introduction: What are pragmatic
alternatives and how can we model
them?
Human communication depends on reasoning not
only about what a speaker said, but also about
what they could have said instead. If you are
thirsty and your friend says, “I only have juice,”
you may infer that your friend does not have wa-
ter, soda, or milk, while remaining uncertain about
unrelated items like soup or batteries. These infer-
ences require listeners to identify relevant alterna-
tives: possible utterances that were available to the
speaker but weren’t said.
Alternatives are central to theories of pragmatic
inference. In theories of focus, focus-sensitive op-
erators such asonlyare standardly analyzed as op-
erating over alternatives to the focused constituent
(Rooth, 1992). Whereas, scalar implicatures are
often analyzed as excluding stronger alternatives
on a preexisting scale, such asallwhen a speaker
sayssome(Grice, 1975; Horn, 1972). Across
these cases, interpretation depends on alternatives.
This raises a computational question, where do the
relevant alternatives come from?
One possibility is that alternatives are taken
from conventional lexical scales or fixed seman-
tic relations. This works well for scalar alterna-
tives such assome/allorgood/excellent, but many
alternatives are context-sensitive. The alternatives
tojuiceafter someone says “I’m thirsty” may in-
clude water or soda, but the alternatives tojuicein
a recipe context, a grocery-store context, or a dis-
cussion of brands will be different. This context-
dependence makes it difficult to specify pragmatic
alternatives using only fixed scales, semantic sim-
ilarity, or lexical category membership.
Recent work has argued that alternatives can be
modeled as probabilistic expectations over what
a speaker could have said. In other words, utter-
ances that are more expected in context are more
likely to be considered in pragmatic inference.
Hu et al. (2023) show that scalar implicatures
vary with expectations over unspoken alternatives.
When a stronger alternative is more expected in
context, listeners are more likely to draw the cor-
responding inference (Hu et al., 2023). Related
work further suggests that variability in pragmatic
inference can arise both from uncertainty about
which alternatives are relevant and from uncer-
tainty about whether those alternatives should be
excluded (Ronai and Xiang, 2024). These findings
support the broad theoretical claim that pragmatic
inference is shaped by expectations over unspoken
alternatives. We build on this claim through the
study of focus alternatives.
In this paper, we propose the computational
claim that in-context next-word prediction is a
mechanism for generating focus alternatives. On
this view, the relevant alternatives to an uttered
item are words or phrases that would have been
likely completions in the same discourse context.
For example, the alternatives tojuicein “I only
have juice” are estimated from a non-focus com-
pletion frame such as “I have .” This estimates
what the speaker could have said before the focus
operator contributes exclusion. The claim is that
context-sensitive expectations over possible con-
tinuations provide a computational mechanism for
generating the alternatives that pragmatic theories
require.
To test this proposal, we separate two compo-
nents of focus alternative-based inference. The
first is alternative generation: assigning candidate
alternatives an expectation score based on how
likely they are in context. The second is alter-
native exclusion: mapping those expected alterna-

2 Experimental Design: How to model
expectations over unspoken
alternatives?
Our goal is to model pragmatic alternatives as ex-
pectations over what a speaker could have said in
context. We build two components: 1) an expecta-
tion model, which assigns probabilities or scores
to possible unspoken alternatives, and 2) a link-
ing structure, which maps those expectations onto
human judgments. In this section, we describe
the expectation model, the linking structures used
to derive pragmatic predictions, and the human
datasets used for evaluation.
2.1 Which language model do we use to
model human next word predictions?
We instantiate expectations over unspoken alterna-
tives using next-word probabilities from Qwen2-
7B. We use LLMs as a computational model of hu-
man next-word prediction for two reasons. First,
methodologically, human next word prediction
judgments (cloze probabilities) are difficult to ob-
tain for low probability words, and these words
could be among the alternatives people consider.
LLMs allow us to get a full next-word probability
distribution across an entire vocabulary. Second,
while the mechanisms driving human next-word
prediction remain unknown (Ryskin and Nieuw-
land, 2023), LLMs provide, at least, a mathemat-
ically transparent model of how next-word dis-
tributions are calculated from context. Note that
our claim of in-context next-word prediction as a
mechanism for generating alternatives is not com-
mitted to a specific next word prediction model nor
is this argument making any mechanistic claims
about how the next word prediction mechanism in
humans or models works.
Because our hypothesis depends on the qual-
ity of the expectation model (i.e. a good model
for human next-word prediction will be a good
model for human alternative generation), we first
checked how well several LLMs approximate hu-
man cloze distributions. For each context, we
compared LLMs next-word probabilities (logits)
to human cloze probabilities using Spearman rank
correlations. We evaluated models on both our
own cloze data, collected from the contexts used in
the focus-alternative study, and an external cloze
dataset from Peelle et al. (2020). Across both
datasets, Qwen produced the highest correlation
with human cloze expectations (Table 1), so we
use Qwen as the primary expectation model in the
experiments below. Full details of prompting, can-
didate filtering, and correlation calculations are re-
ported in AppendixTODO.

The model has two components. The first com-
ponent is alternative generation. Given the dis-
course context with a non-focus completion frame,
the model assigns each candidate alternative an ex-
pectation score corresponding to how likely that
candidate would have been as a continuation in
the context. In the running example, the relevant
expectation is not the probability of water in iso-
lation, but the probability of water as a comple-
tion of the thirsty context. This allows alterna-
tives to vary with context. Water may be highly
expected in a thirst/fridge context but not in a
recipe or shopping-mall context. The expectation
model assigns each candidateqa scoreE(q, c),
corresponding to how likelyqwould have been as
something the speaker could have said in context.
A key feature of the model is that expectations
are estimated from a non-focus completion frame,
while human judgments are measured from an ut-
terance that contains the relevant pragmatic trig-
ger. For example, in the focus-alternative study,
expectations are computed from a frame such as
"I have ", but judgments are collected for ut-
terances such as "I have only juice." This separa-
tion allows us to estimate which alternatives were
available before the focus operator contributes ex-
clusion. Prompts such as "I have only juice but not
" are not used to estimate alternatives, because
they already encode the exclusion relation that the
model is intended to predict.
After estimating expectations, we need a link-
ing structure that maps expected alternatives onto
predicted inferences. We compare four such struc-
tures. These structures instantiate different hy-
potheses about how graded expectations over pos-
sible utterances become judgments about which
alternatives are excluded.
Relation to prior work.The alternative exclu-
sion structures we compare are computational ide-
alizations of recurring ideas in theories of alterna-
tives. First, alternatives are often treated as mem-
bers of a contextually restricted set. This idea
is seen in alternative semantics for focus (Rooth,
1992, 2016) and in structure-sensitive approaches
to alternatives for focus and scalar implicature
(Katzir, 2007; Fox and Katzir, 2011). Second, al-
ternatives are often ordered by strength, informa-
tiveness, or pragmatic utility, as in work on scalar
implicature, Horn scales (Grice, 1975; Horn,
1972, 1989), and probabilistic rational speech act
models (Frank and Goodman, 2012; Goodman and
Stuhlmüller, 2013; Goodman and Frank, 2016).
Our instantiations of alternative structures take in-
spiration from these ideas.
Set.The set structure assumes that sufficiently
expected candidates are treated as relevant alter-
natives. A queried alternativeqis predicted to
be excluded ifE(q, c)exceeds a threshold in the
next word probability distribution. Ifqexceeds a
threshold in the probability distribution, thenonly
texcludesq; otherwise, the utterance leavesqun-
certain.
Ordering.The ordering structure assumes that
listeners compare the queried alternative to the
item the speaker actually said. A queried alterna-
tiveqis predicted to be excluded ifqwas more ex-
pected than the uttered itemt, that is, ifE(q, c)>
E(t, c). Ifqwas more expected thant, thenonlyt
excludesq. Ifqwas less expected thant, thenqis
not excluded byonlyt. Theoretically, why would
the speaker say the less expected thing if the more
expected thing were true?
Conjunction.The conjunction structure is a hy-
brid of the set and ordering structures. A queryq
is excluded byonlytifboththe set and ordering
criteria hold. A queried alternative must be both
sufficiently expected and more expected than the
trigger.
Disjunction.The disjunction structure is a sec-
ond hybrid of the set and ordering structures. A
queryqis excluded byonlytifeitherthe set or
ordering criterion holds. A queried alternative is
excluded either because it is part of the relevant
set or because it outranks the trigger.

3 Scalar Alternative Replication: Can
the model replicate know scalar
alternative effects?
Before testing the model on focus alternatives,
we first ask whether it reproduces known scalar-
alternative effects. This provides a benchmark be-
cause prior work has already shown that scalar
implicature can be captured through expectations
over unspoken alternatives. If our model captures
this mechanism, then alternatives that are more ex-
pected in context should be more likely to be ex-
cluded.
3.1 Does the model reproduce scalar
implicature results?
We first evaluate the model on the scalar inference
data from Hu et al. (2023). Hu et al. showed that
scalar implicature rates vary with the expectedness
of stronger alternatives. When a stronger alterna-
tive is more likely as something the speaker could
have said, listeners are more likely to infer that
the stronger alternative is false. We test whether
Qwen-derived expectations recover this pattern.
For each item, we use Qwen to estimate the ex-
pectedness of the relevant stronger alternative in
context. The model prediction is that higher ex-
pectation for the stronger alternative should corre-
spond to higher human inference rates. This anal-
ysis tests the generation component of the model:
whether in-context prediction identifies the alter-
natives that are likely to enter scalar inference.

3.2 Does the model capture scalar
implicature effects due to QUD and only
manipulations?
Next we evaluate the model on the scalar-
alternative data from Ronai and Xiang (2024).
This dataset is useful because it separates two
sources of uncertainty in scalar inference: un-
certainty about which alternatives are relevant,
and uncertainty about whether those alternatives
should be excluded. Contextual manipulations of
the question under discussion affect which alter-
natives are available, while the presence ofonly
affects whether relevant alternatives are obligato-
rily excluded.
For each item, we compute Qwen expectations
for both the uttered scalar termtand the queried
alternativeq. We evaluate two model variants. The
expectation model predicts that the probability of
excludingqincreases withE(q, c). The order-
ing model predicts exclusion when the queried al-
ternative is more expected than the uttered item,
E(q, c)> E(t, c). This allows us to test whether
scalar judgments depend not only on the expected-
ness of the alternative itself, but also on the rela-
tion between the alternative and what was actually
said.

3.3 Summary
The scalar analyses establish that the model re-
covers previously documented expectation effects
in pragmatic inference. This is important because
scalar alternatives provide an external benchmark:
the model is not first tuned to the focus-alternative
dataset. In the next section, we apply the same
framework to focus alternatives, whereonlypro-
vides a more direct test of which expected alterna-
tives are treated as excluded.
